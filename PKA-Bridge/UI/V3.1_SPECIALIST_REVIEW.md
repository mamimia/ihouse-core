# V3.1 Specialist Cross-Review
## Domaniqo / iHouse Core Operational Surfaces

---

## Review Summary

V3.1 represents a coherent refinement across all five operational surfaces — moving from structural clarity (V3) toward visual density, field-tool authority, and role-specific operational confidence. The CSS tightening is consistent (16px padding, 10px gaps, 3px accents), typography is unified (Manrope headers, Inter body), and the component language (alert cards, stream tasks, settlement blocks) is applied systematically. However, the review surfaces critical integration gaps and role-boundary misalignments that V4 must address.

---

## Surface-by-Surface Review

### OPS_MANAGER V3.1

**Sonia (Operational UX Architect):**
The ops manager surface achieves correct structural separation: it is a supervision dashboard, not a worker task board. The morning briefing (KPI strip, day progress bar, active alerts) is appropriately front-loaded, and the operations stream (cleaning/check-in/maintenance tasks segmented by type) shows field status without pushing tactical detail up. Strength: alert intervention chains are numbered sequentially, signaling a clear command pathway. Concern: the cockpit (analytics view with KPI grid) is positioned as a fifth nav item but its operational purpose is ambiguous — is it for trend analysis or real-time dispatch coordination? Recommend clarifying whether analytics serve supervision or executive reporting; if both, split into two surfaces to maintain boundary clarity.

**Talia (Product Interaction Designer):**
State-to-UI mapping is clean: task cards show SLA countdown, team cards show load bar + status ring, and alert cards are color-coded (red for LIVE, yellow for APPROACHING). The "View Intervention" call-to-action is action-oriented, not passive. However, an edge case surfaces: what happens when an ops manager opens an intervention chain (e.g., "Guest Arrival Blocked") and the blocker resolves in the background while they're still viewing it? The detail view shows property, guest impact, and timeline, but there's no indication of live state refresh or resolution notification. Recommend: add a "This situation has been resolved" banner if the task completes mid-view, with an option to return to the hub.

**Nadia (Chief Product Integrator):**
The OPS_MANAGER surface claims to display real-time KPI data (24 total, 18 done, 4 at-risk, 2 overdue) and a stream of active operations by task type. Integration verification needed: (1) Does the `/ops` endpoint return a task aggregate with completed/at-risk/overdue counts in this shape? (2) Are stream task cards pulling from a filtered task query or from a dedicated ops-stream API? (3) The team cards show load bar and status ring — these require worker availability data. Is `worker_availability` or shift data wired to the ops surface, or is the load calculation UI-side? Status: PARTIAL — the surface is visually complete, but backend contract alignment for real-time KPI refresh is unconfirmed.

**Larry (Chief Orchestrator):**
Cross-domain coherence assessment: OPS_MANAGER correctly separates from ADMIN (no access control, intake review, or settings config visible here) and from individual WORKER surfaces (no task-level detail or photo flows). The role boundary is sound. However, a cross-cutting risk: the morning briefing shows "Day Progress: 18 / 24 completed" as of a snapshot time. If a task completes while the page is open, does the KPI strip auto-refresh? If not, there's silent staleness. If so, how frequently? Ops managers need reliable refresh semantics to make sound decisions. Recommend: define a refresh cadence (e.g., 30-second polling for status changes, real-time for new alerts) and communicate it visually (e.g., "Data as of 9:44 AM" with a refresh timestamp).

### GUEST_PORTAL V3.1

**Yael (Guest Experience Architect):**
The guest portal V3.1 achieves hospitality-first framing: the welcome hero is dominant, stay dates are prominent (7 nights, April 5-12), and the four quick-access cards (Wi-Fi, house guide, check-in, messaging) are visually equitable. The role accent (warm forest green) is consistently applied. Strengths: Wi-Fi display shows network name and password in a premium keycard style; house guide is structured (check-in, check-out, house rules, appliances); messaging is available for host contact. Gaps: (1) the check-in card exists but the guest check-in form flow is not shown — what happens when the guest taps "Check In"? Does it trigger a QR scan, a form entry, or a form link? (2) No post-checkout state is shown — can guests re-access the portal after checkout, and what should they see? (3) No empty-state handling: if Wi-Fi details haven't been configured by the owner, does the portal show "Not configured" or hide the section? Recommend: show empty-state messaging (e.g., "Wi-Fi details will be available soon") rather than silent missing content.

**Sonia (Operational UX Architect):**
The guest portal is correctly scoped as a separate surface from operational role surfaces. It is guest-centric, not role-gated. However, the structural boundary between guest portal and property info must be clear: the house guide (rules, appliances, check-in/check-out times) is property-specific content, not per-booking data. If the owner has not configured house guide content, does the portal show a blank section or a call-to-action? Currently ambiguous. Recommendation: for V4, define a property-content completeness model so the portal can gracefully handle under-configured properties without leaving guests confused.

**Talia (Product Interaction Designer):**
The guest check-in form (referenced but not shown in the portal mockup) requires interaction specification. Current state: the portal shows "Check In" as a card, but the actual form submission flow is undefined. Questions: (1) Does the guest fill out a form (name, passport, companions) within the portal itself, or do they tap a link that opens an external form? (2) If within the portal, what states exist: pre-filled (guest name from booking), empty-on-arrival, or pre-populated from an identity-verified data source? (3) Error handling: if the guest submits incomplete passport info, what is the error message and recovery path? (4) Success state: does the form auto-dismiss, show a confirmation, or persist? Recommend: model the complete check-in form interaction in a dedicated flow spec before V4 implementation.

### CHECK_IN_STAFF V3.1

**Hana (Staff Operations Designer):**
The check-in staff surface V3.1 shows strong operational scaffolding: the day health strip displays a progress bar + "5 of 8 completed" counter, making the check-in worker's daily output visible at a glance. Arrival cards are property-focused (property name 13px/800, property-dominant layout), with guest secondary, which is correct — the worker is checking in a property, not a guest. The wizard is step-gated (1 of 7, 2 of 7, etc.), and settlement policy is surfaced at the top. Strengths: the arrival queue is clear about timing ("Next: 2h 15m", "Opens: 1h 30m"); the worker knows what's available and what's locked. Concern: the wizard shows "Step 1: Confirm Arrival" → "Step 3: Meter Reading" — where is Step 2? If steps are conditional (e.g., meter reading only for metered properties), that conditional logic must be visible to the worker to avoid confusion. Recommend: annotate conditional steps with a sub-label (e.g., "Step 3 (Metered Properties): Meter Reading") so the worker understands branching.

**Sonia (Operational UX Architect):**
The check-in surface is appropriately distinct from the cleaner, checkout, and ops-manager surfaces. Its scope is narrow: confirm arrival, collect deposit (conditionally), read meter (conditionally), capture photos (conditionally), obtain identity, agree to settlement policy. The "Operational Confidence" section notes that the wizard avoids colored pills and board aesthetics in favor of field-ops presentation — this is correct visual differentiation. However, the phrase "Settlement policy & identity steps feel more informational/guest-facing" is concerning. Check-in is a staff surface, not a guest surface. If settlement policy preview is being shown to the worker for clarity, that is appropriate. If it is being verbally relayed to the guest through the worker, that is guest-portal scope. Recommend: clarify whether the "informational/guest-facing" language describes worker clarity or guest communication — and if the latter, ensure the guest check-in form (Yael's scope) is aligned.

**Victor (Financial Lifecycle Designer):**
The check-in staff surface displays settlement policy upfront ("Deposit: ฿10,000 Cash, Electricity: Metered") and includes a step for deposit collection. Settlement preview accuracy requires verification: (1) Is the displayed deposit amount pulled from `booking_financial_facts` or from a property-level default? (2) If a guest has contested the deposit, does the surface show the disputed amount or the default? (3) After collection, the deposit record is written to `guest_deposit_records` (noted as PARTIAL in Hana's role notes). Verify that the worker's confirmation ("Deposit collected") actually persists the record before the worker advances to the next step. Current status: UI is coherent, but backend wiring for deposit persistence must be confirmed as PROVEN before V4. Recommendation: add a feature flag for deposit collection until persistence is wired; show "Deposit preview (not yet collecting)" as a read-only summary until enabled.

### CHECK_OUT_STAFF V3.1

**Hana (Staff Operations Designer):**
The checkout surface V3.1 is well-structured for field-worker confidence: departure cards show property name dominant, guest secondary, and clear overdue/pending/in-progress status badges. The day health strip has been redesigned with a copper border accent to show progress visually ("8 departures today • 2 overdue (+45min)"). **Victor (Financial Lifecycle Designer):**

The net return amount is displayed in copper, 13px/800 weight, signaling financial authority. Critical verification: (1) Does the settlement summary calculate the correct net amount after deductions? **Talia (Product Interaction Designer):**

Questions: (1) Can the worker add a Checkout photo if one does not exist (i.e., first arrival at this step), or must a photo already be present? (2) If no Checkout photo exists, what is the tab content? (3) The damage/issue reporting section shows "Condition Assessment: All Good / Issues Found" radio buttons — if Issues Found is selected, what is the form? (5) State transitions: what happens if the worker clicks "Continue → Meter" on step 1 but the meter hasn't been read yet? Does the next step become blocked, or does the system skip it? Recommend: model state-based branching for the meter step and conditional damage-assessment form before implementation.

## Cross-System Alignment Assessment

**Larry (Chief Orchestrator):**

### Navigation Patterns
All five surfaces use a bottom nav bar (except Guest Portal, which uses a back arrow for nav). The nav icons are consistent:
- OPS_MANAGER: 🏠 Hub, 🚨 Alerts, 📊 Stream, 👥 Team, 📈 Cockpit
- CHECK_IN_STAFF: 🏠 Home, 📋 Work, ✓ Tasks, ⚙ Settings
- CHECK_OUT_STAFF: 🏠 Home, 🚪 Check-Out, ✓ Tasks, ⚙ Settings
- CLEANER: 🏠 Home, 🧹 Clean, ✓ Tasks, ⚙ Settings
- GUEST_PORTAL: ← (back nav), no bottom nav

**Assessment:** The worker surfaces (check-in, checkout, cleaner) use role-specific nav labels ("Work", "Check-Out", "Clean") while keeping Home, Tasks, Settings constant. This is coherent. OPS_MANAGER deviates (no Tasks tab; has Stream and Cockpit instead), which is appropriate for a supervisory surface. GUEST_PORTAL has no persistent nav, which is correct — guests don't navigate between operational domains. Alignment is sound.

### Typography
All operational surfaces use:
- Headers: Manrope, 13px or higher, 800 weight
- Body: Inter, 11px, 500 weight
- Labels: Manrope, 12px, 700 weight, uppercase, 0.5px letter-spacing

**Assessment:** Consistent across all surfaces. This is a strength — visitors can immediately recognize Domaniqo operational tools vs. guest-facing surfaces (which use warmer, softer typography).

### Color Usage
- Operational surfaces: system neutrals (grays, blacks) with accent colors per role/urgency
  - OPS_MANAGER: Red for LIVE alerts, yellow for APPROACHING
  - CHECK_IN_STAFF: Green for ready, blue accents
  - CHECK_OUT_STAFF: Copper (#B56E45) for active states, progress, settlement authority
  - CLEANER: Green for completion, photo badges
- GUEST_PORTAL: Warm forest green (#2D5A3D) throughout, premium luxury treatment

**Assessment:** Operational surfaces follow a system-of-systems color model (red/yellow/green for urgency, copper for authority/financial, neutral base). GUEST_PORTAL breaks the pattern intentionally to signal a different domain. This is appropriate cross-domain distinction, but V4 should document the color semantics to prevent future inconsistencies. Recommend: define a "Domaniqo Operational Color Language" spec that all V4+ surfaces inherit.

### Urgency Visualization
- OPS_MANAGER: Numbered intervention chains, color-coded alerts, SLA countdown on stream tasks
- CHECK_IN_STAFF: Time gates ("Next: 2h 15m", "Opens: 1h 30m"), step counter (1 of 7), conditional step labeling (inferred)
- CHECK_OUT_STAFF: Overdue badges (+45 min), early departure banner (EARLY DEPARTURE at 10:30), copper border for progress
- CLEANER: Active card prominence, completion gates (Items / Photos / Supplies checkboxes)

**Assessment:** Urgency signals are role-appropriate but not uniform. OPS_MANAGER uses temporal signals (SLA countdown). Workers use time gates or completion gates. This divergence is justified by role — ops managers monitor clock time; workers execute discrete tasks. However, a cross-cutting risk: when a worker's task approaches a time deadline (e.g., check-in must complete by 3 PM), does the CHECK_IN_STAFF surface escalate the visual urgency? Currently unclear. Recommend: add a time-urgency escalation pattern (e.g., yellow border at 30 min before deadline, red at 10 min) that all worker surfaces inherit.

### Card Patterns
All surfaces use cards with consistent interior structure:
- Card header: role-specific label + status badge (e.g., "Villa Orchid" + "PENDING")
- Card body: secondary info (guest name, checklist count, etc.)
- Card footer: call-to-action (e.g., "Start →", "View Intervention", "View Details")

**Assessment:** Pattern is consistent across OPS_MANAGER, CHECK_IN_STAFF, CHECK_OUT_STAFF, and CLEANER. Card density is tightened in V3.1 (16px padding, 10px gaps). Touch targets are 44pt (ops worker surfaces) to 48pt (guest portal). This is appropriate. However, the "call-to-action" footer varies: some cards say "Start →", others "View Intervention", others "Continue → Meter". Recommend: standardize CTA language to one of (a) "Start", (b) "Next", (c) "View", (d) "Begin" — pick one per surface to reduce cognitive load.

### Header Treatment
- OPS_MANAGER: "Domaniqo Ops Manager" with a settings button (⚙️) and KPI strip (24 TOTAL, 18 DONE, 4 AT RISK, 2 OVERDUE)
- CHECK_IN_STAFF: "Domaniqo · Check-In" with day health progress bar (5 of 8 completed)
- CHECK_OUT_STAFF: "Domaniqo · Check-Out" with day health strip (8 departures today • 2 overdue)
- CLEANER: "Domaniqo · Cleaning" with clean count (5 cleans today)
- GUEST_PORTAL: "Villa Cielo" (property name) with welcome hero, no system header

**Assessment:** All operational surfaces lead with the Domaniqo brand + role name. Worker surfaces add a day-health metric (progress bar or count) to signal daily workload at a glance. OPS_MANAGER adds a KPI grid instead of progress bar, appropriate for supervision. GUEST_PORTAL omits the system header entirely, correctly emphasizing the property/stay over the Domaniqo platform. Consistency is strong. Recommend: maintain this header pattern in V4; ensure day-health metrics auto-refresh as tasks complete.

### Cross-Domain Risk Summary
1. **Deposit persistence:** CHECK_IN_STAFF claims deposit collection; backend wiring is PARTIAL. V4 must prove persistence before shipping.
2. **Photo sharing across staff roles:** CHECK_OUT_STAFF requires check-in photos to be visible to checkout worker. Integration must confirm this sharing contract.
3. **Settlement calculation:** CHECK_OUT_STAFF displays settlement summary; the calculation engine (damage deduction, account routing) is PARTIAL. V4 must implement backend settlement service.
4. **Real-time refresh cadence:** OPS_MANAGER shows KPI snapshots (18 / 24 completed as of 9:44 AM). Define and implement a refresh strategy (30-second polling? real-time SSE?) before V4.
5. **Guest check-in form workflow:** GUEST_PORTAL claims "Check In" capability but the form interaction is undefined. V4 must specify and implement this flow before shipping.
